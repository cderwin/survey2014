Groton Census: Welcome

For the first time ever, The Circle Voice has launched an extensive survey of the student body at Groton — online! The survey’s 50 questions cover a variety of topics both often discussed and seldom mentioned, ranging from grade inflation and hook-up culture to self-harm and sleep deprivation.

As we have promised, presented here are all of your answers as fully disclosed as anonymity concerns would allow. Please keep in mind that though we did our best, some questions may not have been optimally worded and this is not a scientific survey. The results are divided into six sub-sections: General Information, Academics, Residential Life, Diversity, and Sex and Drugs & Wellness.

Through the hard numbers, we hope to present the collective experiences and opinions that define life on the Circle in the most objective and informative way possible.

The online medium allows for a more in-depth presentation of results than the print edition, and we encourage you to explore the results through these interactive graphs.

We received an incredible number of honest, thoughtful comments, and you can read some of them under Comments. The discussion doesn’t stop there: feel free to provide any additional thoughts and feedback under the Discussion section.

General

In a mere three days, 294 students filled out the survey, a tremendous turnout rate of 79% (91% of whom answered every question). Below we have in-depth demographic charts of the respondents, showing the distribution of socioeconomic status, sexuality, race, gender, and more within our community.

While among men there was a relatively even distribution of political beliefs, there are 3 times as many liberal as conservative females. Also notable is the fact that 45% of females are apolitical, making that their best represented category.

Unsuprisingly, almost exactly half of Grotonians identify with the middle class, while only 7.5% of respondents identified themselves with the lower class. Be warned: unclear definitions of socioeconomic class may have skewed these results.

More than 30 respondents identified as part of the LGBTQ community, while this can be taken as a triumph of diversity on campus, it another question: how many of those thirty are "out?"

Academics

From III Form to VI Form, the number of credits of credits taken decreases each year. The average time spent on homework per night is 2.7 hours, and we see a drastic increase from IV Form to V Form. Evidently, Senior Spring is as rumored. Science, History, English, and Classics are the most time-intensive departments, while Math and Foreign Language are evidently less so.

Females have a higher GPA than males overall, though they do have every student who reported having below an 80. By race, Asians typically outperformed their white and black counterparts, and other races were omitted due to concerns of anonymity and insufficient data.

Though there were more responses asserting that Groton had deflated grades than inflated grades, over 50% of respondents were neutral, perhaps indicating satisfaction with the current state of affairs.

In general, there are more students who agree with Groton’s academic requirements than those who do not. The one notable exception is ethics: though the majority of students in the Vth form and below believe ethics to be a necessary curricular element, 52% of VIth formers, the only students who have taken Ethics, either “Disagree” or “Strongly Disagree.” This may suggest that the Ethics curriculum as it exists may need examination in order not to disappoint so many VIth formers.

Perhaps unsurprisingly, those with lower grades reported a greater disparity in perceived grading fairness, and likewise those with higher grades perceived those grades to be more fair. Interestingly, the three departments in which students reported investing the most time were also those reported as the least likely to reflect that effort in grades.

Campus Life

  • Caffeine Use
  • Overcommitment
  • Extracurricular Motivation

Almost half of respondents reported getting 7 hours of sleep per night and only 4 people reported getting more than 8 hours of sleep per night. Nearly a third of Grotonians never use caffeine, while more than 17% of us use caffeine on a daily basis. More than a quarter of us join clubs primarily to bolster their college application, though more than 60% of us join clubs primarily for extracurricular interest.

With the exception of weekend religious services, with which the majority of participants disagreed, the net results are positive for all social requirements at Groton.

50% of Groton students would first go to a peer when faced with a problem in the dorm. However, more people would first turn to themselves than would turn to peer counselors and faculty members combined.

70% of respondents believe the DC is fair, while slightly more than 30% believe otherwise.

The vast majority of Grotonians are happy at Groton, with only 12% of respondents saying that they are unhappy. Additionally, happiness increases by form until happiness peaks at V form, before decreasing in student's final year at Groton. Almost three-quarters of us would choose to come to Groton if we were to do it again, and only 7.8% would decline to return.

Diversity

  • Racism
  • Sexism
  • Homophobia

34% of Caucasians and 43% of Asians agree or strongly agree that there is racism on campus, compared to 63% of Hispanics and 65% of Blacks.

A far more drastic distinction exists for the opinion on sexism. Only 43% of male students agree that sexism exists on campus, while that same statistic is a staggering 90% for girls.

For homophobia, a similar pattern presents itself: though more students agree than disagree that it exists on campus, that opinion is far stronger among those closest to the issue. 86% of the LGBTQ community at Groton, 32 students, agreed, while only 2 students disagreed.

In a similar way, those who are questioning on our campus were most concerned about the difficulty of coming out on campus -- more so even than those who have decided that they are homosexual or bisexual.

This is the first year of the Diversity and Inclusion Task Force’s existence, so some leeway must be afforded to them regarding the number of students who think they haven’t affected campus life. However, among boys, more responded “negative” than “positive” by a small margin, an interesting result. This will be a question to keep an eye on in future years, as the task force becomes more established on campus.

A general increase in drug use each year is clearly apparent. Less than a quarter of VI formers have not consumed alcohol, and there is a large difference between on-campus alcohol consumption by this year's V and VI forms. Notably, the IV Form chews the most tobacco, while this year's V Form smokes the most, but almost exclusively off-campus. Like alcohol, marijuana sees a large difference in use between this year's V and VI forms. Only V and VI Formers experimented with hard drugs, and they only did so at relatively low rates.

Female students are at risk for self-harm, eating disorders, and major depression:

Female students are 2.7 times as likely to inflict self-harm as males

Females are 4.3 times as likely to experience an eating disorder as males

Females are 2 times as likely to experience major depression as males

1 out of every 5 students has experienced eating disorder

Sex

Males feel less pressured into hookups than females. The most common answer for males was that they disagreed that they were pressured into hookups, while most females were either neutral of agreed.

Fifty-five percent of respondents believe that the campus culture does not foster healthy relationships. Twenty percent more females disagree or highly disagree that campus fosters healthy relationships than males. Notably, only 3 respondents highly agreed that the campus encourages healthy relationships.

Female students tend to have more hookups and relationships than males. The number of hookups and relationships per year increases by form. Notably, relationships in VI form tends to become more steady as the number of 1 relationship increases.

After the age of consent, the incidences of sexual intercourse and oral sex increase dramatically both on and off campus. An overwhelming majority of males have watched porn, while slightly less than 20% of females have. A significant gap exists between Lower School and Upper School males, possibly due to dorm structure. Meanwhile, females tend to watch more porn as they rise in form.
